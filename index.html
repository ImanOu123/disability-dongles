How Not To Make Bad Assistive Tech: A Guide

Created by Iman Ouzzani, Advised by Frank Elavsky

Some technologies that are created with "good" intentions can end up effectively useless, sometimes even harmful. This can especially be true when technology is made for people with disabilities. So how do you avoid making bad assistive technologies? We created this guide and framework to help you ask questions about your own work and suggest directions for how you can rethink, reframe, and adjust your approach.

Motivation: Avoiding Disability Dongles

Consider the following examples of bad assistive technology:

Sign-Language Glove

SignAloud - The Index Project

Why is it bad? The people who make these projects rarely consult with deaf people when they create it, which results in gloves that make an assumption that sign language only occurs in the movement of the hands, rather than with facial expressions and body language as well.

Stair-Climbing Wheelchair

Scewo BRO - Scewo

Why is it bad? These wheelchairs are often very expensive. For example, the Scewo BRO has an average selling price of €45,000 (or over $50,800 USD). Furthermore, if the goal is to create more accessible pathways, there is a cheaper and more effective low-tech alternative that can serve a larger group of people: building more ramps.

Liz Jackson, an advocate for disability-led design practice defined this type of technology as the disability dongle. Disability dongles are assistive technologies created to solve "a real or perceived problem experienced by disabled people, but which was built without including disabled people in the design process."

Disability Dongle rhetoric instills in students the value of a quick fix over structural change, thus preventing them from seeking out, participating in, and contributing to existing inquiry."
Liz Jackson - Disability Dongle

What is "structural change"?

    Ableism is the “the discrimination of and social prejudice against people with disabilities based on the belief that typical abilities are superior” and often relies on the idea that people with disabilities need 'fixing' (Ableism 101).

    Ableism, like many other forms of oppression, is often embedded in the various systems and institutions that make up a society such as within the government, healthcare or education. It exists in the form of “rules, policies, and procedures” that “stigmatize, exclude, or harm people with disabilities” (Systemic Ableism).

    In this context, Liz Jackson distinguishes between quick fixes and structural change, because quick fixes are often bandage solutions to these issues that can only be fixed with structural change. Structural change is when you identify the root of the problem within a system or institution and address it from there.

    For example, the stair-climbing wheelchair acts as a quick fix for the issue of inaccessible pathways, whereas the structural change would be advocating for more ramps and policy that would ensure more ramps across various areas.

In that vein, we seek to provide a framework that leads designers to ask constructive questions about their work and seek solutions with real impact for people with disabilities.

Who are you?

You are a designer, researcher, engineer or student planning to build or building an assistive technology targeted towards people with disabilities. You want to create the best possible outcome for your technology such that it poses the least amount of harm to the intended users of your product. You will use this tool to identify any potential harm that your product might pose.

How to use?

There are seven considerations that you need to consider when you are building the assistive technology:

  1. Rhetoric
  2. Creation process
  3. Problem definition
  4. Solution definition
  5. Cost
  6. Usage
  7. Impact of technology
Under each of these considerations, there is a series of questions that you answer to the best of your ability to help you interrogate these considerations. You can answer these questions based on what you have already completed in your design process or what you plan to do. It is better to answer these questions as early as possible in the creation process to make it easier to reframe your work, if needed.


Based on your answers to the questions, consider the suggestions.

Considerations

Note the intended users are the people who this assistive technology is meant to or can serve. We don’t use the general term person with disability to describe the user - even though it adds a more 'human' element to describe the audience - to the type of people who assistive technology is made for, because it is too general. For example, there needs to be a distinction between a ‘person with a disability’ being involved in the design process of the technology vs a person with the specific disability that the technology is meant to target.

Rhetoric of Technology

This consideration deals with the way the language is used to describe the technology and the intended users, and whether the language aligns with what is preferred by the intended users.

For rhetoric, the texts that should be analyzed are as follows: For research papers look at the wording in the paper and other supporting material published by the creators. For products, look at the promotional material and other materials published by the creators. Finally, for both projects also look at the informal messaging you use during the creation process (e.g. emails, meetings, etc.)

Questions

  1. Does the messaging around the technology involve harmful terminology? (Answer with reference to UN list of terminology to avoid)
    2. Why?

      The language we use to describe people with disabilities is important because it can reinforce harmful stereotypes or stigmatize individuals. This language is often patronizing in which people with disabilities are looked down upon as different or worthy of less.

      For example the use of the term “wheelchair bound” is commonly rejected because it reinforces the idea that people who use wheelchairs are victims that are confined or restricted to their wheelchair.

  2. Does the messaging around the technology focus more on the innovation than the assistance it provides to the user?
    3. Why?

      Many people who create dongles build an innovative technology and then to ‘give it purpose’ they tack on accessibility towards the end. This is often evident in the language they use to describe the technology, in which innovation comes first and accessibility comes second.

      When it comes to building assistive technology, accessibility must be the primary purpose of the technology since its inception, so the researchers and designers can take the necessary steps to involve people with disabilities in their creation process and create a technology that solves a real problem people with disabilities have.

Suggestions

If the messaging uses harmful terminology...

Reframing the messaging you use, based on the provided resource.

If there is more focus on the innovation of the technology...

Think about what the purpose of creating this technology has been for you. Is it to create something innovative or is it to help people? If innovation takes a higher importance, consider broadening the scope of the technology beyond assistive technology. If helping people is more important, consider focusing more on the benefits and impact the technology has on the intended audience in its messaging.

Guiding Example

  • UN Disability-Inclusive Language Guidelines: An example of harmful language is “suffers from [disability]” because it “suggest constant pain and powerlessness and carry the assumption that persons with disabilities have poor quality of life”. Instead, “has [disability]” is a better term to use.
Suggested Readings
  • Stella Young's TED Talk: This talk tackles the idea of the objectification of people with disabilities as objects of inspiration for other people. This objectification is most noticeable in the rhetoric that is used when describing people with disabilities.

Creation Process of Technology

This consideration is the one of the most important characteristics that can make or break an assistive technology. It deals with whether, when, and how the intended audience is involved in the creation process of the technology.

A lack of involvement from the intended audience will lead to a project that isn’t based on evidence that it is in fact solving a real problem that the intended audience has in a manner that will and can be used by the intended audience.

The following a simplified version of the creation process of the assistive technology:

Questions

  1. For each stage above, highlight the level of involvement of the intended users at each stage

    2. The following are the different possible levels of involvement:
    • Disability-led (at least 1 person with disabilities is in charge and has agency and power)
    • Participatory, Co-design, and/or Disability-centered (at least 1 person with disabilities is treated as an equal to other decision-makers and considered a "subject matter expert" in matters related to their disability)
    • "Inclusive" (person with disabilities are present in some way, but their role isn't explicitly equal in power with decision-makers)
    • Participant/user (typically brought in to evaluate as part of a study or test but don't have active power or agency to make decisions)
    • Not involved at all
  2. How do you intend to compensate the intended users for their participation or leadership in the project?
    3. For example
    • Financial Compensation (i.e. paying them for their time)
    • Academic Compensation (i.e. Co-authorship in published work)

Suggestions

If there is a lack of involvement of people with disabilities in the creation process...

  • If you are at the initial stages of the creation process, plan out in what ways you can involve the intended users into different stages of the creation process. Start by looking into the concepts of co-design and participatory design.
  • If you are in the later stages of the creation process, look into how you can involve the intended users as participants to evaluation studies, preferably during the prototyping stage rather than only at the end. Another helpful resource is to use an accessibility consultant to assess the technology.

Guiding Example

  • Many dongles do not involve people with disabilities at all in the creation process or at a minimum only include them at an evaluation stage towards the end.
Suggested Readings
  • Interdependence as a framework, Bennett et al: A reading on participatory design and co-design.
    "Focusing solely on independence - on whether an individual achieves a task autonomously - leaves out important factors that can help us for example, anticipate the awkward aspects of social interactions and the roles of policy, labor, and materials that shape infrastructural-level decisions"

Problem Definition

This consideration deals with whether the ‘problem’ the technology is trying to solve is a real problem that the intended audience has defined to be such.

Questions

  1. What is the ‘problem’ that the technology aims to solve?
  2. Based on the 'problem' defined, has the ‘problem’ been defined by the intended users?
    1. If so, what evidence can be provided to support this claim?
      2. For example
      • Personal experience i.e. you are a possible intended user for this technology
      • Initial focus groups or interviews with intended users
      • Academic papers published by the intended users or involved initial focus groups with intended users
      • Non-academic sources (blog posts, social media, etc) published by the intended users
    2. If not, who defined the ‘problem’?
      3. For example
      • You i.e. you are not a part of the possible intended users
      • Academic papers not published by the intended users or didn’t involve initial focus groups with intended users
      • Non-academic sources (blog posts, social media, etc) not published by the intended users

Suggestions

If the 'problem' hasn't been defined by the intended audience...

  • Consider finding a co-designer or collaborator from the intended users or finding an accessibility consultant.
  • If that is not possible, consider running a focus group with the intended audience. Otherwise, conduct a literature review with sources from the intended audience that corroborate the claim.

If you cannot complete any of the previous steps:

  • And you are at the initial stages of your creation process (i.e. ideation), consider finding another problem to solve.
  • And you are at later stages of the creation process (e.g. the prototyping phase), consider broadening the scope of the technology beyond assistive technology.
    • Why?

      If this bullet has been reached in the suggestions, then that implies that this technology will not be useful as an assistive technology, because it attempts to solve a problem that people with disabilities don’t actually face.

      Thus, to not completely scrap the prototype created and still be able to show the world its innovation, consider refocusing the technology to be used for another purpose.

Solution Definition

This consideration deals with two possible issues in the way the solution is defined: trying to ‘fix’ someone's disability and the availability of alternative low- or no-tech solutions.

Questions

  1. How does the technology solve the ‘problem’?
  2. Based on the solution defined, does the solution expect the intended user to change something about themselves to fit into how society thinks they should behave?
    Why?

      As explained above, ableism is often rooted in the idea that disabilities need ‘fixing’. This is often presented in dongles that expect users to conform to societal norms.

      For example, autistic people are often the target of these technologies, where they are expected to speak or act differently, because the way they speak or act may be different to what society expects of them.

      Technologies that are then designed for autistic people “correct” their speaking, mannerisms, or other behaviors, in order to conform to a normative set of expectations

  3. Are there other ways the ‘problem’ could be solved without the introduction of a new technology?

Suggestions

If the solution expects the intended user to conform to normative standards...

Think about why a framing such as this is problematic and can enact harm on the intended users. Then, reframe your solution such that it doesn’t have these expectations on its users or consider broadening the scope of the technology beyond assistive technology.

If there are there other ways the ‘problem’ can be solved without the introduction of a new technology...

  • Then, if your ultimate goal is to build a new and innovative technology, consider broadening the scope of the technology beyond assistive technology.
  • Or, if your ultimate goal is to solve the ‘problem’, consider pursuing the low-tech or non-tech solution instead.

Guiding Examples

  • Expectation of conforming to normative standards:

    • Counterventions: a reparative reflection on interventionist HCI: the SayWAT wearable device expects autistic people to conform to normative standards of speech.

  • Availability of low-tech or no-tech solutions:

    • Stair-climbing wheelchairs were created to solve the problem of inaccessible pathways and the lack of ramps. If your ultimate goal is to have more accessible pathways, then rather than looking for new tech to build, look into ways you can increase ramps in your local community through advocacy.

Suggested Readings
  • When the implication is not to design (technology): Suggests questions that HCI researchers and designers should ask themselves about whether the introduction of a new technology is necessary in the context of the problem they are trying to solve.
  • We Need an Interventionist Mindset: Highlights the differences between a solutionist and interventionist mindset when it comes to finding solutions to problems, in which solutionism is rooted in the idea that “a single policy or technology can create permanent closure around an issue.”

Cost of Technology

This consideration deals with how much the technology will cost for the intended users monthly and annually. In addition, it deals with any other unexpected costs the intended users may need to face to maintain the use of the technology.

Note, as it is recommended for this guide to be completed as early as possible in the creation process, answer the questions below with what you expect the costs to be with the information you have available.

Questions

  1. How often does this technology need to be purchased and are there other parts that need to be purchased regularly?
    2. For example
    • One time purchase for everything
    • One time purchase for main component, and weekly/monthly/annual purchases for smaller components
    • Weekly/Monthly/Annual purchases for everything
  2. How much does this technology cost for someone to purchase it initially without any insurance or other assistance?
    • What is the cost per month/year to purchase this technology in its entirety?
  3. For each common insurance company, what is the level of coverage that this technology has and how much would the intended user pay?
    • With this coverage, what is the cost per month/year to purchase this technology in its entirety?
  4. Are there other avenues that this technology can be purchased without insurance at a reduced price or for free?
    • With this assistance, what is the cost per month/year to purchase this technology in its entirety?

Suggestions

If the technology incur costs over a month or year that make it expensive for the intended audience with or without insurance...

Reflect why the technology incurs such costs. Is the technology expensive from the get-go or does it accumulate costs over time? Also, reflect on how these costs will translate into people’s lives. Then, based on these reflections, problem solve where to reduce costs to make it more affordable or how the intended users can use external help such as government/non-government programs that can help people afford it.

Guiding Example

  • RadMad Lab Projects - Mad Lab at Cal: DIY assistive technology is an example of low-cost assistive technology where people use readily available materials to create simple yet useful assistive technology.

Usage of Technology

This consideration deals with how easy it is for the intended users to learn how to use the technology and whether there is enough support from the creators of the technology to help the intended users in doing so.

Questions

  1. What prerequisites must the intended users fulfill in order to be able to use this technology?
    2. For example
    • Basic/Moderate/Advanced level of tech savviness
    • Specific motor skills (e.g. able to push a joystick)
  2. In what ways will the intended users be helped to learn how to use the technology?
    3. For example
    • Instructional videos/pamphlets
    • Sessions with a professional
  3. How will the ‘learning’ materials be accessible to the intended users?
    4. For example:
      The learning material shouldn’t be visual if the technology is targeted for people who are blind or low vision.
  4. Have any studies been conducted to test whether the intended users can easily learn how to use the technology?
  5. Are there any affordances that the technology offers that makes it easy to use?
    6. For example
      It is a technology that is similar to a technology that is part of the intended user's life.

Suggestions

If the technology difficult to learn how to use and/or offers no affordances to the intended users...

  • If you are in an earlier stage of the creation phase (e.g. prototyping phase), consider conducting a series of evaluations with the intended audience or redesigning some of the affordances.
  • If you are in a later stage of the creation phase, consider creating informational material alongside the technology and conducting an evaluation of how well these materials work.
    • For example
      An information material that is a printed paper won't be helpful for someone who is blind. Instead, a better option is material that is offered as an online HTML file or alongside a braille hand guide.

Guiding Example

  • A company produced an adaptive game controller for people with upper body motor impairments, but made the packaging inaccessible. Later due to player response this was changed. If they did testing earlier on with the packaging, they wouldn’t have had this problem.

Impact of Technology

This consideration deals with whether there are any plans for maintaining the technology after it is released to ensure the project is not abandoned after its release.

Questions

  1. What are the next steps that you plan on taking to further develop this technology?
  2. Who will carry out these next steps to further develop this technology?
  3. Who will finance and sustain the production of this technology in the future?

Suggestions

If there is a lack of a plan for how this assistive technology will be sustained in the future...

Think about what your goal for this assistive technology is and whether impact is a part of that goal. Do you truly want this technology to be a part of people’s lives after its inception? If so, consider setting up a detailed plan on how you can continue to develop this technology, finance it and bring it about to the world for people to use. If not, why create it?

Guiding Example

  • A lot of dongles come from student projects, where the student’s intention is to only produce something for the class with no intention of further developing it after the class has ended.
